Booking.com left my children homeless on New Year's Eve – then hung up when I called for help

A mother and three children, aged 5, 8 and 16, were left on the streets of London after a vendor cancelled their accommodation. When a representative called Booking.com's emergency line, forensic audio analysis proves the agent fabricated "audio issues" to end the call

Investigation by Justice Minds Forensic Intelligence

Wed 31 Dec 2025 23.59 GMT

A mother and her three children were left without shelter on New Year's Eve after Booking.com failed to provide relocation assistance. Evidence photograph: Justice Minds Investigation

Samantha Turner had saved for months. The single mother wanted to give her three daughters – Leah, 16, Bibi, 8, and Amaze, 5 – a New Year's Eve they would remember. She booked what was advertised as a "Central London skyline view highest floor service apartment" through Booking.com, paying £536 for two nights.

They travelled from Guernsey by ferry and coach. When they arrived at the Westminster property on 30 December, the door code didn't work. Then came the messages.

Messages from the property vendor

Don't waste your time
You should have been better prepared
I think this is a curse and I would blame all on you
Please don't be over clever
Your child who shouldn't face this issue and wasn't their fault

The vendor informed Turner the property had been "vandalised" and was unavailable. Under Booking.com's own partner terms, vendors are required to "relocate the person to alternative accommodation that's the same or better at no extra cost." No such offer was made.

The family spent the night of 30 December sleeping in the corridor of an art gallery.

The call that proves obstruction

On 31 December, Ben Mak, a forensic legal consultant, encountered the family and attempted to resolve the situation. He called Booking.com's customer service line at 4.28pm. What happened next has been captured in forensic detail.

Booking.com's AI system immediately recognised the severity: "Since this is a serious matter, let me transfer you to a customer service representative."

Then came the wait.

There was no callback. No relocation. No refund release. Three children remained homeless.

A pattern of conduct

This is not an isolated incident. Mak himself experienced strikingly similar treatment from Booking.com in January 2025.

⚠️ Not an isolated incident: Pattern evidence

Less than 12 months earlier, Booking.com inflicted identical harm on another vulnerable consumer. The pattern is undeniable.

27 January 2025 – Liverpool

Ben Mak, an autistic man, attempted to book emergency accommodation on the day of a close friend's funeral. Booking.com took his payment of £32.74 but provided no confirmation, no booking, no service.

  • Disability disclosed: Autism
  • Vulnerability disclosed: Bereavement (funeral day)
  • Mobility impairment: Foot injury
  • Call duration: 3 hours – then terminated
  • Refund offered: 12-day wait
  • Result: Slept rough on Liverpool streets

The repetition of identical conduct across multiple cases—with knowledge of prior harm—transforms individual failures into evidence of systemic corporate policy.

In both cases, Booking.com's system was informed of vulnerability. In both cases, calls were terminated without resolution. In both cases, the consumer was left without shelter.

The legal framework

Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, services must be performed with "reasonable care and skill." Booking.com's own documentation promises that "Customer Service can help" when properties are unavailable.

The evidence suggests Booking.com's agent terminated the call knowing three children would remain homeless as a result. On New Year's Eve, when hotel rates in London exceed £300 per night, the commercial calculation is stark: relocation is expensive; terminating calls costs nothing.

Evidence: Refund Timing Proves Capability (January 2025 Case)

In Ben Mak's January 2025 case, Booking.com eventually processed a refund – but the timing reveals a critical policy failure. The refund was issued after 2:00 AM, long after Mak had been left homeless on the streets of Liverpool. This proves Booking.com can process immediate refunds when they choose to – they simply didn't prioritise it during the crisis.

Ben Mak's Bank Statement – January 2025

Account showing declined transactions – Booking.com had already taken the £32.74, leaving insufficient funds. Multiple failed attempts visible.

Metadata Timestamp – Refund at 02:44 AM

Tuesday 28 Jan, 02:44 – Refund finally processed in the early hours, long after Mak was already sleeping rough.

Policy Implication: If Booking.com can process refunds at 2:44 AM, they can process them at 5:00 PM when a consumer is standing homeless. Their refund policy requires urgent reform to mandate immediate payment release when their platform creates a housing crisis.

The Turner family was eventually housed through the intervention of Mak, who secured emergency accommodation at a hostel for £133 – paid from his own pocket. This was not Mak's responsibility. His intervention came at significant personal cost: impacting his own legal court orders and professional workflow. But for Booking.com B.V.'s failures, there would have been no homeless family on the street and no missed court deadlines.

Legal proceedings against both the property vendor and Booking.com B.V. are now being prepared. The family is seeking recovery of £536 plus damages for distress, consequential losses, and professional intervention costs totalling in excess of £9,000.

A complaint has been lodged with Trading Standards. A referral to Westminster Children's Services under Section 47 of the Children Act 1989 – relating to children at risk of significant harm – is being considered.

Booking.com processes millions of transactions annually. The forensic evidence in this case raises a question the company will need to answer: how many other vulnerable consumers have been terminated on fabricated pretexts?

Three children know the answer cost them their New Year's Eve.
